## [SLIDE 1] Title

### Slide bullets

* Forecasting from distributed information
* Different sources have different skill
* How should a market learn that?

### Script

Good morning. My name is Anastasia Cattaneo.

This thesis studies how to combine predictions from different sources when useful information is distributed across many actors.

That setting is common in energy, logistics, and operations. Different firms, models, or analysts each see part of the signal. They do not all see the same information, and they are not equally reliable. Some sources are consistently stronger than others, and that difference in skill matters for the final forecast.

So the broad question behind this thesis is simple: when predictive information is distributed, how should we combine it, and how should we decide whose forecast deserves more influence?

## [SLIDE 2] Why this problem matters

### Slide bullets

* Decisions depend on good forecasts
* Information is dispersed across actors
* Raw data are often costly to share
* Forecasts can be shared more easily than data

### Script

Forecast quality matters because many decisions are made under uncertainty. In energy, that includes scheduling, balancing, bidding, and reserve planning. In logistics and operations, it includes inventory, staffing, and risk management.

The practical difficulty is that the relevant information is often fragmented. One participant may have a better model. Another may have better local data. A third may adapt faster when conditions change.

At the same time, raw data are often private, commercially sensitive, or simply expensive to share. So in practice, it is often easier to share a forecast than to share the full data and modelling pipeline behind it.

That shifts the design problem. We want a way to extract useful predictive information without requiring full data disclosure.

## [SLIDE 3] Why simple aggregation is not enough

### Slide bullets

* Combining forecasts often improves robustness
* Different forecasters make different errors
* Participants are strategic
* Forecasters are not equally skilled
* Skill changes over time

### Script

A natural response is forecast aggregation.

That usually helps because different forecasters make different errors. One may be biased in one regime, another may react slowly, another may be noisy but occasionally informative. Combining them can improve robustness and average accuracy.

But once forecasts come from strategic participants, aggregation alone is not enough.

Three problems appear immediately. We need participants to take part. We need them to report honestly. And we need a sensible rule for deciding whose forecast should count more.

A repeated setting adds a fourth issue. Forecasters are not equally skilled, and their reliability can change over time. Some adapt quickly. Some become stale. Some perform well only under specific conditions.

That is the central issue in this thesis. A forecasting market should not treat all influence as fixed. It should learn who is reliable.

## [SLIDE 4] Thesis question

### Slide bullets

* Can the market learn reliability?
* Can it use reliability to improve the aggregate forecast?
* Can it preserve the core market discipline?

### Script

This leads to the specific thesis question.

Can a forecasting market learn which participants are reliably accurate, give those participants more influence over time, and improve the aggregate forecast?

And can it do that while preserving the core market discipline that makes the mechanism credible?

That second part matters. I am not only asking for a better weighting rule. I am asking for a repeated market design that remains economically coherent while adapting to differences in skill.

## [SLIDE 5] Where this work sits

### Slide bullets

* Lambert: self-financed wagering — strong one-shot structure
* Raja: forecasting market with client and payoff allocation
* Vitali: adaptation over time, intermittent participation
* Gap: adaptive + self-financed + absolute skill learning

### Script

There are three main pieces of theory behind this work.

Lambert and co-authors give the self-financed weighted-score wagering mechanism. Participants submit a forecast and a wager, the pool is redistributed by relative performance, and the mechanism has strong formal properties. That gives the core settlement structure.

Raja and co-authors turn that into an explicit forecasting market with a client, sellers, aggregation, and payoff allocation. But each instance is still history-free.

Vitali and Pinson move closer to the repeated setting. They allow intermittent participation and adapt weights over time. But their weights are relative — on a simplex — rather than an absolute measure of each participant's reliability.

So the gap is clear. One literature gives strong market structure without adaptation. Another gives adaptation without the same self-financed design. My thesis combines the two.

## [SLIDE 6] My contribution

### Slide bullets

* Self-financed forecasting market
* Online skill-learning layer
* Influence depends on deposit and learned reliability
* Skill is absolute and pre-round

### Script

My contribution is a self-financed forecasting market with an online skill-learning layer.

Each participant still chooses a deposit, but deposit alone does not determine influence. The mechanism first estimates the participant's reliability from past performance, then uses that reliability to decide how much of the deposit should actually count in the round.

That design is motivated by a simple concern. Deposit size alone mixes two different things: financial capacity and forecasting quality. A participant with more money should not dominate the aggregate forecast purely because they can afford a larger deposit.

The skill signal is absolute — it represents the reliability of one participant directly, not their share in a normalised weight vector. It is computed before the round begins, using only past information.

## [SLIDE 7] System and mechanism

### Slide bullets

* Environment: synthetic DGPs + real Elia wind and electricity
* Agents: 7 forecasters — Naive, EWMA, ARIMA, XGBoost, MLP, Theta, Ensemble
* Behaviour: honest, noisy, strategic, adversarial presets
* Core pipeline: submit → skill gate → aggregate → settle → update
* Same effective wager controls weight and exposure

### Script

The system has three layers. The environment defines the data — both synthetic benchmarks for controlled testing and real data from the Elia Belgian grid operator for wind power and electricity prices.

The agent layer generates per-round actions. On the real data, seven forecasting models participate: Naive persistence, an exponentially weighted moving average, ARIMA, XGBoost, a neural network, the Theta method, and a Naive-plus-EWMA ensemble. These are not toy models — they range from simple baselines to machine learning, and their heterogeneity is what gives the mechanism something to learn from.

The behaviour layer can produce honest forecasters or adversarial ones — sybils, arbitrageurs, reputation gamers — without touching the core mechanism.

The core pipeline runs each round in five steps. First, each participant submits a probabilistic forecast and a deposit. Second, the skill gate converts the deposit into an effective wager. Third, effective wagers serve as aggregation weights. Fourth, the market settles after the outcome is observed. Fifth, realised performance updates wealth and skill for the next round.

The key design choice: the same effective wager determines both influence and exposure. You cannot have influence without risk.

## [SLIDE 8] Why the skill layer matters

### Slide bullets

* Skill comes from realised forecasting loss
* Recent performance matters more
* Skill decays during absence
* Prevents stale reputation
* Keeps the market adaptive

### Script

The skill layer is the main innovation.

For each forecaster, the mechanism keeps a running summary of realised forecasting loss. Recent performance receives more weight than distant performance, so the estimate remains responsive when conditions change.

The mechanism also handles intermittent participation. If a forecaster is absent, their skill drifts gradually towards a baseline. That prevents a participant from preserving an old reputation indefinitely without taking further risk.

Skill is not a fixed label. It is a state variable that the market keeps learning over time. Future influence depends on accumulated evidence of reliability.

## [SLIDE 9] Main empirical result

### Slide bullets

* Real-data validation on Elia datasets
* Strong gain on wind
* Small gain on electricity
* Gains depend on forecaster heterogeneity

### Script

The main empirical result is straightforward.

On the Elia wind dataset, the mechanism improves substantially over equal weighting, by about one third.

On the Elia electricity dataset, the improvement is much smaller, at around four per cent.

That pattern is important. The mechanism gains most when there are meaningful differences in forecaster quality for the skill layer to exploit. When the forecasters are already close in performance, the room for improvement is smaller.

The gains are real, but they depend on the structure of the forecasting environment.

## [SLIDE 10] Why the mechanism is credible

### Slide bullets

* Skill recovery is perfect in the controlled benchmark
* Budget balance passes
* Identical sybil splitting gives no gain
* Core mechanism behaves as intended

### Script

Three checks support the credibility of the mechanism.

First, in the controlled skill-recovery benchmark, the learned ranking matches the true ranking perfectly. The learning layer is identifying persistent differences in forecasting quality.

Second, the mechanism passes budget-balance checks in the test suite.

Third, identical identity splitting gives no gain beyond floating-point noise.

These checks do not replace the real-data result, but they support its interpretation. The mechanism is not only producing interesting outcomes — it is behaving in the intended way.

## [SLIDE 11] Interpretation, limitations, and conclusion

### Slide bullets

* Skill helps the aggregate; full mechanism helps more
* Best single can still outperform the aggregate
* Improvement is conditional, not universal
* Tail calibration remains imperfect
* Richer strategic behaviour remains open
* Influence depends on earned reliability

### Script

The wind result should be interpreted carefully.

A skill-only version already improves over equal weighting. The full mechanism improves further. That supports the main claim that learned reliability is genuinely useful for aggregation.

At the same time, the best single forecaster can still outperform the aggregate in some settings. The contribution is not universal dominance — it is conditional improvement in a disciplined market setting.

The limitations are clear. The aggregate does not always beat the best individual. The gains depend on forecaster heterogeneity. Tail calibration needs improvement. And richer strategic behaviour remains open.

To conclude: this thesis develops a self-financed forecasting market with online skill learning. Influence depends on earned reliability rather than deposit alone. The mechanism learns who is reliable, updates that over time, and uses it to improve the aggregate forecast. The answer to the thesis question is yes, but conditionally.
