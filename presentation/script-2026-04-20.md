## [SLIDE 1] Title

### Slide bullets

* Forecasting from distributed information
* How can we combine predictions well?
* How can we reward useful contributions?

### Script

Good morning. My name is Anastasia Cattaneo.

This thesis is about how to combine predictions from different sources when each source may hold useful information, but may not want, or be able, to share its underlying data.

This matters in many practical settings, especially in energy and operations, where decisions have to be made under uncertainty and the relevant information is spread across different actors.

So the broad problem is simple: how can we pool predictive information effectively when the information is distributed?

## [SLIDE 2] Why this is difficult

### Slide bullets

* Useful information is distributed
* Raw data may be private or costly to share
* We want the information, not necessarily the raw data
* This is a forecasting problem and an incentive problem

### Script

The difficulty is that better forecasts often require combining information from multiple sources, but those sources may face strong reasons not to reveal their raw data.

For firms, sharing data can reduce competitive advantage. In other settings, it creates privacy or operational concerns.

So the challenge is not only statistical. It is also institutional. We want a way for participants to contribute useful predictive information and be rewarded for doing so, without requiring full data disclosure. That basic motivation is central in the theory you are building on.

## [SLIDE 3] Why combining forecasts helps, but is not enough

### Slide bullets

* Combining forecasts often improves accuracy
* Different forecasters capture different parts of the signal
* But participants may be strategic
* We need participation, honesty, and sensible weighting

### Script

A natural idea is to combine forecasts.

That often works well because different forecasters capture different aspects of the signal and make different errors. So aggregation can improve robustness and accuracy.

But once those forecasts come from strategic participants, simple averaging is no longer enough.

We need people to participate. We need them to report honestly. And we need a sensible rule for deciding whose forecast should count more. So this becomes a mechanism-design problem, not only a forecasting problem.

---

## [SLIDE 4] Thesis question

### Slide bullets

* Existing mechanisms often use static influence
* But forecaster reliability changes over time
* Can the market learn who is reliable?
* Can it use that to improve the aggregate forecast?

### Script

This leads to the specific question I study.

In many existing mechanisms, influence is effectively static. It depends on something fixed, such as stake, or each round is treated independently.

But in repeated forecasting settings, forecasters are not equally reliable, and reliability can change over time.

So the specific question in this thesis is: can a forecasting market learn which participants are reliably accurate, give them more influence, and improve the aggregate forecast over time?

And the second part of the question is equally important: can it do that without breaking the core logic of the market mechanism?

## [SLIDE 5] Where this sits in the literature

### Slide bullets

* Lambert: self-financed truthful wagering
* Raja: client, aggregation, and payout architecture
* Vitali: adaptation and intermittent participation
* Missing piece: adaptive self-financed mechanism with absolute skill

### Script

There are three main pieces of theory behind this work.

First, Lambert and co-authors give the self-financed weighted-score wagering mechanism. This is the one-shot theoretical core: participants submit forecasts and wagers, the pool is redistributed based on relative performance, and the mechanism has strong formal properties.

Second, Raja and co-authors turn that core into a more explicit forecasting market. A client posts a forecasting task, sellers submit forecasts and wagers, the market aggregates those forecasts, and then allocates payoffs. But the framework is history-free. Each market instance is treated independently.

Third, Vitali and Pinson introduce adaptation over time and allow intermittent participation. That is much closer to realistic repeated forecasting, but the learned weights are relative weights in a constrained combination system, rather than an absolute notion of each participant’s reliability.

So the gap is clear: one literature gives strong market structure without adaptation, and another gives adaptation without the same self-financed mechanism design. My thesis combines the two.

## [SLIDE 6] Core idea

### Slide bullets

* Keep the self-financed wagering core
* Add online skill learning
* Convert deposit into effective exposure
* Influence depends on both commitment and reliability
* Skill is absolute, not just relative

### Script

The core idea is to keep the self-financed wagering structure, but add a history-aware skill layer.

Instead of letting deposit or stake alone determine influence, the mechanism uses past forecasting performance to decide how much of a participant’s deposit should actually count in the round.

That creates what I call an effective wager. A participant may deposit a certain amount, but only a skill-adjusted share of that amount determines both how much their forecast influences the aggregate and how much money they truly place at risk.

This matters because stake alone can confound wealth and information quality. A rich but inaccurate forecaster should not dominate simply because they can afford a large deposit. My mechanism is designed to reduce exactly that problem.

## [SLIDE 7] How one round works

### Slide bullets

* Submit probabilistic forecast and deposit
* Apply skill gate
* Aggregate using effective wagers
* Settle the market
* Update wealth and skill for the next round

### Script

One round has five steps.

First, each participant submits a probabilistic forecast and chooses a deposit.

Second, the mechanism applies a skill gate. If past performance has been strong, most of the deposit becomes active market exposure. If past performance has been weak, only a smaller share becomes active and the remainder is refunded.

Third, the aggregate forecast is formed using those effective wagers as weights.

Fourth, once the outcome is observed, the round is settled using the weighted-score wagering logic.

Fifth, realised performance is fed back into the learning layer, which updates each participant’s skill for future rounds.

The key design point is that the same effective wager governs both influence and exposure. You cannot obtain influence without taking risk.

## [SLIDE 8] Skill learning and intermittent participation

### Slide bullets

* Skill comes from realised forecasting loss
* Recent performance matters more than distant performance
* Absent forecasters decay towards baseline
* Prevents reputation freezing
* Absolute skill rather than simplex-style relative weights

### Script

The skill signal is the central innovation.

For each forecaster, the mechanism keeps a running summary of past forecasting loss. Recent performance matters more than distant performance, so the mechanism can adapt when conditions change.

If a participant is absent, their skill does not remain frozen indefinitely. It gradually decays towards a baseline. This is important because otherwise someone could perform well, leave the market, and preserve a high reputation without taking further risk.

This is also the key difference from relative-weight approaches. In those systems, weights are normalised against one another. If one forecaster’s weight rises, another’s must fall. In my mechanism, skill is absolute. It is meant to represent the reliability of one forecaster, not just their share of a fixed weight vector.

A useful way to state the logic is this: past performance determines how much exposure you are allowed to deploy this round, while current-round performance determines whether you win or lose this round. That is why this is not double counting.

## [SLIDE 9] Main empirical result

### Slide bullets

* Elia wind, 7 forecasters
* Equal weights: 0.0679646
* Mechanism: 0.0450420
* Improvement: 33.7%
* Skill-only: 0.0478516
* Best single: 0.0362669
* Electricity gain smaller: 3.77%

### Script

This is the main empirical result.

On the Elia wind dataset, with seven forecasters, the equal-weight baseline has mean CRPS 0.0679646.

My full mechanism reduces that to 0.0450420. That is an absolute reduction of 0.0229227, which corresponds to about a 33.7% improvement over equal weights.

A skill-only variant reaches 0.0478516, so the full mechanism also improves on using skill alone.

At the same time, the best single forecaster still performs better, with mean CRPS 0.0362669. I present that explicitly because it makes the result more credible. The mechanism improves over equal weighting, but it does not dominate every alternative.

On the Elia electricity dataset, the gain is much smaller, around 3.77% relative to equal weights.

So the interpretation is conditional. The mechanism helps when there is meaningful heterogeneity among forecasters, and helps less when the forecasters are already similar. That is, in my view, the correct academic conclusion.

## [SLIDE 10] Why the mechanism works

### Slide bullets

* It must identify who is actually better
* Skill-recovery benchmark
* Perfect rank recovery
* Supports the interpretation of the real-data result

### Script

A natural question is why the mechanism improves the aggregate forecast in some settings.

The answer is that the learning layer appears to identify persistent quality differences correctly.

In the controlled skill-recovery benchmark, with six forecasters and fixed nuisance conditions, the mechanism recovers the true quality ranking perfectly in both point and probabilistic settings. The reported Spearman correlation is 1.0000 for both realised loss ranking and learned skill ranking.

This matters because it shows that the skill signal is not arbitrary. The mechanism improves when it has something real to learn, namely persistent differences in forecaster quality.

## [SLIDE 11] Sanity checks and robustness

### Slide bullets

* Budget balance passes in tests
* Identical identity splitting gives no gain
* Skill is computed before the round
* Robust in baseline cases, not fully attack-proof

### Script

I only keep one brief slide on sanity checks.

First, the mechanism passes budget-balance checks in the test suite at tolerance 10^{-10}, so it behaves as self-financed in code.

Second, identical identity splitting gives no gain. In the sybil split experiments, profit ratios are essentially one, with deviations around 10^{-16}, which is only floating-point noise.

Third, the timing is important: the skill score is computed before the round, from past information only. That preserves the intended logic of the mechanism and avoids using within-round information to set within-round influence.

So the claim is not that the mechanism is immune to every possible strategic adversary. The claim is narrower and more defensible: the mechanism is internally coherent and robust in the main baseline cases.

## [SLIDE 12] Conclusion

### Slide bullets

* Self-financed forecasting market with online skill learning
* Influence depends on earned reliability
* Strong gain on wind, modest gain on electricity
* Gains depend on forecaster heterogeneity
* Best single can still win
* Calibration and richer adversaries remain open

### Script

To conclude, this thesis develops a self-financed forecasting market with online skill learning.

The central idea is to let influence depend not only on deposit, but on earned reliability.

The main result is that this can improve aggregate forecasting. On Elia wind, the mechanism improves substantially over equal weights. On Elia electricity, the gain is smaller. So the right conclusion is not universal superiority, but conditional improvement when there is enough heterogeneity to exploit.

At the same time, the best single forecaster can still outperform the aggregate, so this is not a dominance claim.

The main open issues are calibration, especially in the tails, and robustness against richer adaptive adversaries. In your own notes, tail miscalibration is the clearest modelling weakness, so that is the right limitation to state explicitly.

So the answer to the thesis question is yes, but conditionally: a market can learn who is reliable and use that to improve the aggregate forecast, while keeping the core self-financed mechanism structure intact.
