## [SLIDE 1] Title

### Slide bullets

* Forecasting from distributed information
* Different sources have different skill
* How should a market learn that?

### Script

Good morning. My name is Anastasia Cattaneo.

This thesis studies how to combine predictions from different sources when useful information is distributed across many actors.

That setting is common in energy, logistics, and operations. Different firms, models, or analysts each see part of the signal. They do not all see the same information, and they are not equally reliable. Some sources are consistently stronger than others, and that difference in skill matters for the final forecast.

So the broad question behind this thesis is simple: when predictive information is distributed, how should we combine it, and how should we decide whose forecast deserves more influence?

---

## [SLIDE 2] Why this problem matters

### Slide bullets

* Decisions depend on good forecasts
* Information is dispersed across actors
* Raw data are often costly to share
* Forecasts can be shared more easily than data

### Script

Forecast quality matters because many decisions are made under uncertainty. In energy, that includes scheduling, balancing, bidding, and reserve planning. In logistics and operations, it includes inventory, staffing, and risk management.

The practical difficulty is that the relevant information is often fragmented. One participant may have a better model. Another may have better local data. A third may adapt faster when conditions change.

At the same time, raw data are often private, commercially sensitive, or simply expensive to share. So in practice, it is often easier to share a forecast than to share the full data and modelling pipeline behind it.

That shifts the design problem. We want a way to extract useful predictive information without requiring full data disclosure. This motivation is central in the forecasting-market literature you build on.

## [SLIDE 3] Why simple aggregation is not enough

### Slide bullets

* Combining forecasts often improves robustness
* Different forecasters make different errors
* Participants are strategic
* Forecasters are not equally skilled
* Skill changes over time

### Script

A natural response is forecast aggregation.

That usually helps because different forecasters make different errors. One may be biased in one regime, another may react slowly, another may be noisy but occasionally informative. Combining them can improve robustness and average accuracy.

But once forecasts come from strategic participants, aggregation alone is not enough.

Three problems appear immediately. We need participants to take part. We need them to report honestly. And we need a sensible rule for deciding whose forecast should count more.

A repeated setting adds a fourth issue. Forecasters are not equally skilled, and their reliability can change over time. Some adapt quickly. Some become stale. Some perform well only under specific conditions.

That is the central issue in this thesis. A forecasting market should not treat all influence as fixed. It should learn who is reliable.

## [SLIDE 4] Thesis question

### Slide bullets

* Can the market learn reliability?
* Can it use reliability to improve the aggregate forecast?
* Can it preserve the core market discipline?

### Script

This leads to the specific thesis question.

Can a forecasting market learn which participants are reliably accurate, give those participants more influence over time, and improve the aggregate forecast?

And can it do that while preserving the core market discipline that makes the mechanism credible?

That second part matters. I am not only asking for a better weighting rule. I am asking for a repeated market design that remains economically coherent while adapting to differences in skill.

## [SLIDE 5] Theoretical starting point

### Slide bullets

* Lambert: self-financed weighted-score wagering
* Forecast plus wager
* Settlement by relative performance
* Strong economic structure

### Script

The first theoretical foundation is Lambert and co-authors.

Their weighted-score wagering mechanism provides a very clean one-shot design. Participants submit a forecast and a wager. After the outcome is observed, the wager pool is redistributed according to relative forecast performance.

Why is that important? Because it gives a strong market structure. The mechanism is self-financed, so it does not need an outside subsidy. It also has a clear incentive logic under the standard assumptions.

For this thesis, Lambert gives the core settlement structure and the basic idea that forecasting influence can be tied to an economic commitment. 

---

## [SLIDE 6] What later work adds

### Slide bullets

* Raja: explicit forecasting market with client, aggregation, and payoff allocation
* Vitali: adaptation over time and intermittent participation
* Remaining gap: skill-aware self-financed mechanism

### Script

Two later strands are especially important.

Raja and co-authors turn the one-shot wagering idea into an explicit forecasting market. There is a client, there are sellers, the market aggregates probabilistic forecasts, and payoffs are allocated after the outcome is known. That gives a richer market architecture, but each forecasting instance is still essentially history-free. 

Vitali and Pinson move much closer to the repeated setting. They allow intermittent participation and adapt weights over time. That is important because real forecasters do not appear in every round, and conditions change. 

The remaining gap is the one this thesis targets: an adaptive, repeated forecasting market that keeps the self-financed wagering logic while learning who is reliable.

## [SLIDE 7] My contribution

### Slide bullets

* Self-financed forecasting market
* Online skill-learning layer
* Influence depends on deposit and learned reliability
* Skill is absolute and pre-round

### Script

My contribution is a self-financed forecasting market with an online skill-learning layer.

Each participant still chooses a deposit, but deposit alone does not determine influence. The mechanism first estimates the participant’s reliability from past performance, then uses that reliability to decide how much of the deposit should actually count in the round.

That design is motivated by a simple concern. Deposit size alone mixes two different things: financial capacity and forecasting quality. A participant with more money should not dominate the aggregate forecast purely because they can afford a larger deposit.

So the central object in my mechanism is learned forecaster skill.

That skill signal is **absolute**. It is meant to represent the reliability of one participant directly, rather than their share in a normalised weight vector. It is also computed **before** the round begins, using only past information.

## [SLIDE 8] Mechanism overview

### Slide bullets

* Submit forecast and deposit
* Apply skill gate
* Create effective wager
* Aggregate with effective wagers
* Settle and update

### Script

The mechanism works round by round.

First, each participant submits a probabilistic forecast and chooses a deposit.

Second, the mechanism applies a skill gate to that deposit. If past performance has been strong, a large share of the deposit becomes active market exposure. If past performance has been weak, a smaller share becomes active and the remainder is refunded.

Third, the aggregate forecast is formed using these effective wagers as weights.

Fourth, once the outcome is observed, the market settles using the weighted-score wagering rule.

Fifth, realised performance updates both wealth and skill for the next round.

The key design choice is that the **same effective wager** determines both influence and exposure. A participant cannot gain forecasting influence without also taking economic exposure.

## [SLIDE 9] Why the skill layer matters

### Slide bullets

* Skill comes from realised forecasting loss
* Recent performance matters more
* Skill decays during absence
* Prevents stale reputation
* Keeps the market adaptive

### Script

The skill layer is the main innovation.

For each forecaster, the mechanism keeps a running summary of realised forecasting loss. Recent performance receives more weight than distant performance, so the estimate remains responsive when conditions change.

The mechanism also handles intermittent participation. If a forecaster is absent, their skill drifts gradually towards a baseline. That keeps the skill estimate current and prevents a participant from preserving an old reputation indefinitely without taking further part in the market.

This point is important conceptually. Skill is not treated as a fixed label. It is treated as a state variable that the market keeps learning over time.

That makes the mechanism dynamic in the precise sense that matters here: future influence depends on accumulated evidence of reliability.

## [SLIDE 10] Main empirical result

### Slide bullets

* Real-data validation on Elia datasets
* Strong gain on wind
* Small gain on electricity
* Gains depend on forecaster heterogeneity

### Script

The main empirical result is straightforward.

On the Elia wind dataset, the mechanism improves substantially over equal weighting, by about **one third**.

On the Elia electricity dataset, the improvement is much smaller, at around **four per cent**.

That pattern is important. It suggests that the mechanism gains most when there are meaningful differences in forecaster quality for the skill layer to exploit. When the forecasters are already close in performance, the room for improvement is smaller. 

This is the right level of conclusion for the talk. The gains are real, but they depend on the structure of the forecasting environment.

## [SLIDE 11] Why the mechanism is credible

### Slide bullets

* Skill recovery is perfect in the controlled benchmark
* Budget balance passes
* Identical sybil splitting gives no gain
* Core mechanism behaves as intended

### Script

Three checks support the credibility of the mechanism.

First, in the controlled skill-recovery benchmark, the learned ranking matches the true ranking perfectly. That shows the learning layer is identifying persistent differences in forecasting quality. 

Second, the mechanism passes budget-balance checks in the test suite. 

Third, identical identity splitting gives no gain beyond floating-point noise. 

These checks do not replace the real-data result, but they support its interpretation. They show that the mechanism is not only producing interesting outcomes. It is also behaving in the intended way.

## [SLIDE 12] Interpretation, limitations, and conclusion

### Slide bullets

* Skill helps the aggregate; full mechanism helps more
* Best single can still outperform the aggregate
* Improvement is conditional, not universal
* Tail calibration remains imperfect
* Richer strategic behaviour remains open
* Influence depends on earned reliability

### Script

The wind result should be interpreted carefully.

A skill-only version already improves over equal weighting. The full mechanism improves further. That supports the main claim that learned reliability is genuinely useful for aggregation. 

At the same time, the best single forecaster can still outperform the aggregate in some settings.

That is an important point to state clearly. The contribution of the mechanism is not that it always beats every alternative. The contribution is that it improves the aggregate forecast in a disciplined market setting, especially when there is enough heterogeneity for the market to learn from.

The limitations are clear.

The aggregate forecast does not always outperform the best individual forecaster.

The gains depend on there being meaningful heterogeneity among participants.

Calibration, especially in the tails, still needs improvement.

And richer forms of strategic behaviour remain an open problem.

These limitations matter because they define the scope of the result. The mechanism is useful, but it is not the final word on repeated forecasting markets.

To conclude, this thesis develops a self-financed forecasting market with online skill learning.

The central idea is simple: influence should depend on earned reliability rather than deposit alone.

The mechanism learns who is reliable, updates that estimate over time, and uses it to shape the aggregate forecast.

Empirically, this improves over equal weighting in meaningful settings, with the strongest gains where forecaster heterogeneity is largest.

So the answer to the thesis question is clear: a forecasting market can learn who is reliable and use that information to improve the aggregate forecast over time.
